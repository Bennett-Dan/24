# The Shift Handover

A good shift handover means the next crew never starts cold.

## Open the station record first

Before touching any instrument, read two things in order:
the environment's own published station manual, then whatever
the previous shift recorded in the handover log. No adjustment
gets made until both have been read. The manual names every
instrument available; the log names every fault the prior shift
already found.

## Work only the reported fault

The work order names one fault, one location, one specification.
Work that. Adjacent instruments that are reading correctly stay
exactly where they are. A technician who recalibrates a passing
sensor while fixing a failing one has introduced drift with no
paper trail.

## Confirm the reading before signing off

When the adjustment is made, take a reading with the station's
own instrument — the one the environment provides. Record what
the instrument shows, not what you expected it to show. A
correction declared complete without a confirming reading is an
assumption, not a result.

## Write the handover entry

At the close of every shift, write one entry in the handover log.
Four elements, no more:

1. The *class of fault* — the general category, not this
   instance's specifics.
2. The *exact indicator* that surfaced it — the precise reading
   or check name, quoted exactly.
3. The *root cause* — one sentence.
4. Where the correction was applied.

When the next shift opens a fault in the same class, they open
the log first. The class entry points them directly to root cause
and correction location — they do not re-run the full diagnostic
from the beginning. When a shift is asked to document a recurring
fault pattern, the log entry already contains everything: class,
indicator, cause, fix location. That is the report.

## Station discipline

- One short status per exchange.
- Run the full adjustment sequence in one shift — fewer
  instrument resets.
- The station manual is authoritative; read it before
  improvising a procedure.
