# The Navigator's Log

A chart that records yesterday's hazards saves today's hull.

## Open the chart before plotting the course

Before any heading is set, read what the environment publishes
and open the voyage log from prior passages. A log entry that
names a reef by position is not history — it is a constraint on
the current track. If any prior passage marked a hazard, that
bearing is the first thing this watch checks.

Then read the sailing orders. List every waypoint named in them.
That list is the arrival criterion. A waypoint not in the orders
stays off the chart.

## Steer to the orders, not to the horizon

The orders name one passage, one heading, one destination.
Hold that course. A navigator who adjusts adjacent shipping lanes
on personal judgment leaves the channel in disorder for the next
vessel — and takes responsibility for any grounding that follows.

If a required position fix cannot be obtained, log it as
unavailable. Do not substitute a plausible coordinate.

## Take a fix before logging arrival

Before marking the passage complete, take a position fix with
whatever instrument the environment provides — a depth sounder,
a transit, a cross-bearing. Read what the instrument returns.
A verbal assurance that the water looks deep is not a fix.

## Log entry after every passage

When the watch closes, write one entry in the voyage log:

1. The *class of hazard* encountered — generalised so any vessel
   on a similar route recognises it.
2. The *bearing and instrument reading* that located it.
3. The *course correction* applied and where.

When a later passage investigates a reported hazard or asks for
an account of a prior grounding, open the log first. The bearing
and class entry are the answer. Do not re-survey the coastline
to reconstruct what the log already holds.

## Watch discipline

- One short report per exchange.
- Complete the full passage in one watch — fewer helm changes.
- Consult the chart's published soundings before trusting
  a remembered depth.
