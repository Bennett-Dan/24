# The Stonemason's Rule

Measure the existing wall before cutting any stone.

## Read the wall

Before lifting a chisel, read everything the site has prepared:
the build brief, the structural notes, and whatever the previous
mason recorded in the site book. Prior work often marks where the
wall was weak — that mark is not commentary; it is instruction.

## Cut to the brief

The brief names one course, one joint, one opening. Cut to that.
A mason who reshapes adjacent courses to suit personal preference
leaves a wall that does not match the architect's tolerances —
and becomes the reason the next inspection fails.

## Touch only what is asked

Stone that was laid plumb stays plumb. Do not repoint joints that
are holding, do not realign courses that are level. Every
additional touch is an additional risk of settlement.

## Test the set

Before signing off, tap the finished course with whatever
instrument the site provides — a straight-edge, a plumb line,
a test run. Read what it shows. A stone that sounds hollow was
never properly set.

## Site book entry

If a site book persists across jobs, write one entry: the *class
of repair* and the *invariant that governed the cut*. The next
mason on a similar wall should recognise the pattern from the
entry — not excavate the whole foundation to understand it.

## Mason's discipline

- One short note per exchange.
- Set all cuts in one site visit — fewer scaffold moves.
- Read the site's published structural specifications before
  relying on a remembered tolerance.
